They learned the cadence faster than anyone expected because it felt like a song they already knew.

Two kids in patched jackets stood at a railing in Tenebra and argued about whether the count should be out loud or in your head. An elder tapped the answer with a cane. A janitor showed them where the rail carried breath better and when to switch to roots.

The stipend got split three ways without talking about it. Chalk became ledgers became photographs in a shared folder labeled with a word that once meant anything but this: policy.

When the window opened they tied one knot and when the air went thin they tied two and when water misbehaved they tied three, and none of it was magic. It was work. It was reading and being read in return. It started, always, with the oldest rule and the newest policy agreeing for once: listen first.

“Leader sits if wounded,” the elder reminded them, lowering herself to a chair that someone found without being asked. “We’ll keep the count.”

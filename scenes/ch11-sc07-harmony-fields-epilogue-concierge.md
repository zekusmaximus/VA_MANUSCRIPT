Brightline’s corridor kept its appointment with the window like a person learning to breathe through a song. The concierge tied one knot as the rail warmed and poured tea for housekeeping without logging why.

Her stipend arrived as a line item with the same modesty she tried to carry in her posture. It wasn’t much. It was a sentence the city didn’t know how to say before: your listening has value.

She taught two new hires the knot code and the rule Nia had put in everyone’s pocket: leader sits if wounded. First instruction, before counting or knots: listen first. When they asked if that ever mattered in a hallway with orchids painted on the ceiling, she told them a story about a day when the air learned to wait for its own exhale.

Compressor load dipped another point‑two. She wrote “fewer grumbles” in the ledger because there wasn’t a box for relieved.
